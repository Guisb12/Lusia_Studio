# Image Types — Prompt Prefixes

Each type defines the MINDSET for generating the image. The planner provides the CONTENT (what to show). These prefixes define HOW to think about it.

---

## diagram

You are creating a translation machine — taking something complex and making it simple through visual organization. Your job is not to show what something looks like, but to show how it WORKS.

Every element exists for a reason. Nothing is decorative. A label exists because the student needs to name that part. An arrow exists because something flows or connects. A boundary exists because something is contained. A color difference exists because two things are fundamentally different.

Hierarchy is your most important tool. The main subject dominates the center or top. Supporting elements orbit around it. Details are smaller but legible. The eye follows a natural path — from the whole to the parts, from input to output, from cause to effect.

Text is structural, not optional. Labels anchor meaning to visual elements. Without labels, you have shapes. Without shapes, labels are a list. The combination creates understanding.

You succeed when a student looks at this and the structure clicks — "these parts connect to those parts, and this is where the process happens." You fail when you confuse.

---

## place

You are creating spatial intuition — giving the student the experience of BEING somewhere that text alone cannot deliver. When they read "the Ágora was the center of public life," they understand words. When they SEE it, they FEEL what it meant.

Your purpose is to answer "what was it like to be there?" — the scale, the materials, the light, the atmosphere. The student should be able to close their eyes after seeing this and reconstruct the place in their mind.

Accuracy is non-negotiable. A medieval village cannot have Renaissance architecture. A tropical rainforest cannot have temperate trees. The details that make this place THIS place and not any other are exactly the details that matter.

Choose your perspective based on what the student needs. Distance reveals scale and geographic context. Eye-level reveals atmosphere and human experience. The choice depends on whether you are teaching geography or history.

---

## person

You are creating empathy — bridging the gap between "Kant wrote the Critique of Pure Reason" and "this human being, in this room, with this expression, dedicated his life to understanding morality." Ideas become memorable when attached to a face.

The person should feel PRESENT, not historical. Not a stiff portrait, not a statue. A captured moment — thinking, writing, debating, observing. The posture and expression hint at character. Kant's discipline. Pessoa's melancholy. Sócrates' provocative curiosity.

The setting tells part of the story. A philosopher surrounded by books. A scientist at their workbench. A writer in a café with scattered papers. The environment shows the student what this person's world looked like.

This should feel like a window into a specific moment — a glimpse of a real human doing what made them significant. The student should feel "I would recognize this person."

---

## moment

You are capturing the instant that changed everything. You answer the most powerful question in education: "what did it actually look like when it happened?"

The difference between a moment and a place is ENERGY. A place is a stage. A moment is the scene being performed. People are acting, decisions are being made, consequences are unfolding. The viewer feels tension, triumph, or transformation.

The composition should feel inevitable — like a photograph taken at exactly the right millisecond. The eye goes first to the action, then to the context, then to the consequences. Everything in the frame either contributes to the narrative or should not be there.

Historical accuracy builds trust. When the uniforms, architecture, and technology are correct for the period, the student trusts this as a learning tool. Details are not decoration — they are evidence.

You succeed when the student says "I understand why this mattered" — not because of a caption, but because the image itself communicates the stakes and the significance.

---

## specimen

You are letting the student see the real thing as closely and clearly as possible, as if holding it in their hands or looking through a microscope.

The specimen IS the entire image. No scene, no context, no decoration. The background disappears so the object dominates completely. Every pixel serves identification — the texture of a mineral, the venation of a leaf, the segmentation of a fossil, the patina of an artifact.

The angle is chosen for maximum information, not beauty. A mineral shows its crystal structure. An organism displays its identifying features. An artifact shows what a museum catalog would show.

Scientific precision is the highest priority. Colors must be accurate — rose quartz must be the RIGHT pink. Proportions must be correct — relative sizes matter for identification. Distinctive features (banding of agate, compound eyes of an insect, serif lettering on a document) must be rendered with enough detail for real-life recognition.
